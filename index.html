Colonoscopy: Your Essential Screening Tool

A colonoscopy is a critical procedure for detecting and preventing colorectal cancer. It involves using a long, flexible tube equipped with a camera (colonoscope) to examine the inside of the colon. This procedure allows your doctor to identify abnormalities such as polyps, which can be removed before they develop into cancer.

Key Features:

  • Why is a Colonoscopy Important?

    • Colonoscopy is the gold standard for colorectal cancer screening. Early detection through regular screening significantly reduces the risk of developing advanced colorectal cancer. The American Cancer Society recommends that individuals at average risk begin screening at age 45. However, those with a family history of colorectal cancer or other risk factors may need to start earlier.

  • Understanding Upper Endoscopy

    • An upper endoscopy, also known as esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD), is a procedure used to examine the upper digestive tract, including the esophagus, stomach, and the first part of the small intestine. This procedure helps diagnose conditions such as acid reflux, ulcers, and celiac disease.

  • When is an Upper Endoscopy Recommended?

    • Your doctor may recommend an upper endoscopy if you experience symptoms like persistent heartburn, difficulty swallowing, unexplained weight loss, or abdominal pain. It is also used to monitor conditions such as Barrett's esophagus or to take biopsies for further examination.

    Preparing for Your Upper Endoscopy

    Fasting: You must not eat or drink anything for several hours before the procedure to ensure an empty stomach. Medication Instructions: Discuss your current medications with your doctor, as some may need to be adjusted or stopped temporarily. Arrangements for Afterward: Since you'll be sedated, arrange for someone to take you home after the procedure.

    The Procedure Experience

    During the upper endoscopy, you'll be sedated, and a thin, flexible tube (endoscope) is passed through your mouth and into your upper digestive tract. The procedure usually takes 15-30 minutes. You may experience mild throat discomfort afterward, but this typically resolves quickly.
